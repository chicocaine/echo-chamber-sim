## Quick Orientation

This simulation models how **echo chambers form on social media** — the process by which people gradually end up only seeing and believing content that reinforces what they already think.

The simulation runs in discrete time steps called **ticks**. On each tick:
- Each agent sees a **content feed** generated by a recommender algorithm
- Each agent **updates their opinion** based on what they see and who they follow
- The simulation logs **metrics** that measure how polarized or clustered the network has become

All of this happens on a **social graph** — a network of agents connected by follow/friend relationships. The structure of that graph, and how it changes over time, is just as important as the agents themselves.

## Part 1 — Agents

### 1.1 Agent Types

There are eight agent archetypes. Every agent in the simulation is one of these types. Types are assigned at initialization and do not change mid-run (unless specified).

---

#### Zealot
- **What they do:** Hold a fixed extreme opinion and never, ever change it. They are the immovable anchors of the simulation.
- **Influence on others:** Yes — neighbors can be pulled toward a zealot's opinion over time.
- **Key trait:** `stubbornness = 1.0`
- **Opinion:** Fixed at initialization, typically at one extreme (e.g., −1.0 or +1.0 on a [−1, 1] scale)
- **Use case:** Seeding ideological poles; modeling hardline activists or committed partisans

---

#### Stubborn Agent (Friedkin–Johnsen)
- **What they do:** Update their opinion by blending their *original* belief with influence from neighbors. They move, but they always feel the pull of where they started.
- **Key trait:** `self_weight g_i ∈ (0, 1)` — higher = more anchored to initial belief
- **Update rule:** `x_i(t+1) = g_i · x_i(0) + (1 − g_i) · weighted_avg(neighbors)`
- **Typical default:** `g_i ≈ 0.1–0.3` (mildly stubborn)
- **Use case:** Most realistic model of typical social media users with some prior commitment

---

#### Flexible Agent (DeGroot)
- **What they do:** Fully average the opinions of their neighbors. No memory of their starting position. Pure social learner.
- **Key trait:** `g = 0` (no self-weight)
- **Update rule:** `x_i(t+1) = Σ a_ij · x_j(t)` where `a_ij` are normalized influence weights
- **Convergence:** Will reach consensus if the influence graph is strongly connected
- **Use case:** Baseline agent; good for testing whether network topology alone creates echo chambers

---

#### Bounded-Confidence Agent (Hegselmann–Krause)
- **What they do:** Only listen to neighbors whose opinions are *close enough* to their own. Ignore everyone else entirely.
- **Key trait:** `confidence_bound ε ∈ [0, 1]` — the maximum opinion gap they'll tolerate
- **Update rule:** Average only neighbors `j` where `|x_j(t) − x_i(t)| ≤ ε`
- **Typical default:** `ε ≈ 0.3`
- **Effect:** Low ε → opinions cluster into isolated groups. High ε → eventual consensus.
- **Use case:** Modeling ideological silos; testing how tolerance levels affect polarization

---

#### Contrarian
- **What they do:** Intentionally push back against their social environment. When peers lean one way, they lean the other.
- **Key trait:** `contrarian_probability p_c ∈ [0, 1]` — chance of inverting the influence signal on any given tick
- **Behavior:** With probability `p_c`, adopt the *opposite* of what neighbors would normally push them toward
- **Use case:** Modeling devil's advocates, political contrarians, or "reply guys"

---

#### Influencer
- **What they do:** Operate like a Flexible or Stubborn agent in terms of opinion updates, but have outsized reach due to their position in the network.
- **Key trait:** High node degree; optionally a boosted `influence_weight` so their posts carry more persuasive force
- **Network position:** Hub node — many incoming and outgoing connections
- **Use case:** Modeling celebrities, journalists, political figures, or viral accounts

---

#### Passive / Quiet Agent
- **What they do:** Exist in the network but rarely act. Low probability of posting or engaging each tick.
- **Key trait:** `activity_rate` — very low (e.g., 0.05–0.1 actions per tick vs. 0.5–0.9 for active agents)
- **Still update:** They do update opinions when they consume content, just infrequently
- **Use case:** Modeling lurkers; representing the silent majority of social media users

---

#### Misinformation Bot
- **What they do:** Automatically generate and spread false content. They do not update their own opinions — they are purely output machines.
- **Key traits:**
  - `activity_rate` — very high (posts multiple times per tick)
  - `misinfo_rate` — fraction of posts that are misinformation (often 1.0)
  - `opinion` — fixed, typically extreme
  - `stubbornness = 1.0`
- **Detection signals:** Abnormally high post frequency, near-zero opinion variance, high sharing of high-valence content, low reciprocity ratio
- **Use case:** Modeling coordinated inauthentic behavior, troll farms, spam accounts

## Part 2 — Psychological Mechanisms

These are the cognitive biases that govern *how* agents respond to content. They are not separate modules — they are baked into the update rules and feed selection logic.

### 2.1 Confirmation Bias & Selective Exposure
Agents have a higher probability of engaging with content that aligns with their current opinion. Implemented via the similarity score in the recommender (see Part 4). The closer a piece of content's `ideological_score` is to the agent's `opinion`, the more likely the agent is to select and process it.

### 2.2 Disconfirmation Bias
When agents encounter content that contradicts their opinion, they weight it less. Operationalized by scaling the influence of counter-attitudinal content by `(1 − susceptibility)` before applying the opinion update.

### 2.3 Social Conformity
Agents feel pressure to align with their local peer group. This is the core mechanism of the DeGroot and Friedkin–Johnsen update rules — agents literally average toward their neighbors.

### 2.4 Emotional Contagion
High-emotion content spreads faster and is processed differently. When an agent consumes a high-valence content item, their `emotional_arousal` spikes, which: (a) increases their sharing probability, and (b) *narrows* their effective confidence bound, making them less receptive to opposing views. See Part 5 for the exact equations.

### 2.5 Motivated Reasoning
Agents interpret ambiguous content in the direction of their existing opinion. Not a separate variable — it is captured by the asymmetric weighting of pro-attitudinal vs. counter-attitudinal content in the similarity scoring function.

### Misinformation Credibility Spectrum

| misinfo_score Range | Category | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| 0.0 – 0.2 | Credible | Verified; from trusted sources |
| 0.2 – 0.5 | Contested | Disputed; evidence is mixed |
| 0.5 – 0.8 | Misleading | Partially false; missing context |
| 0.8 – 1.0 | False | Fabricated; debunked |

### How misinfo_score Affects Agent Belief Updates
An agent updates their belief in content according to:

```
belief_update_weight = (1 − misinfo_score) × source_credibility × (1 − (1 − media_literacy_i) × misinfo_score)
```

A credible source with a misleading claim (score 0.6) can still shift a low-media-literacy agent significantly.

## Part 4 — Recommendation Algorithms

The recommender decides what content each agent sees each tick. It is the primary mechanism through which echo chambers form algorithmically.

### The Key Parameter: Personalization Strength α

`α ∈ [0, 1]` controls how much the recommender prioritizes content similar to the agent's preferences.
- `α = 0` → fully random feed (no echo chamber effect from algorithm)
- `α = 1` → fully personalized feed (maximum filter bubble)
- **Default for "filter bubble" simulations:** `α ≈ 0.6–0.7`

### Similarity Scoring Formula

Before content is ranked for a given agent, each candidate content item gets a similarity score:

```
sim(c, i) = α_t × cos(topic_vector_c, pref_vector_i)
          + α_id × (1 − |ideological_score_c − opinion_i|)
          + α_s × (1 − |sentiment_c − mood_i|)
```

where `α_t + α_id + α_s = 1` (tunable blend weights). The `pref_vector_i` is the agent's accumulated topic preference, updated via exponential moving average on each engagement.

### Recommender Types

| Type | How It Works | Echo Chamber Tendency | Key Parameters |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Content-Based** | Scores items by similarity to agent's past likes | High — narrows over time | α blend weights |
| **Collaborative Filtering** | Recommends what similar users liked | High — creates user-cluster loops | neighborhood size k, latent factors |
| **Hybrid** | Combines content-based + CF | Medium — depends on diversity enforcement | blend ratio |
| **Graph-Based** | Random walk / PageRank on user–item graph | Medium-high — can trap in dense clusters | restart probability, walk length |
| **Reinforcement Learning** | Learns from user feedback; balances explore/exploit | Tunable — can reward diversity explicitly | learning rate, exploration rate, reward function |

## Part 5 — Opinion Update Rules

These are the mathematical rules that determine how an agent's opinion changes each tick. Each agent type uses one of these rules.

### DeGroot (Flexible Agents)

```
x_i(t+1) = Σ_j  a_ij × x_j(t)
```

`a_ij` is the normalized influence weight agent `i` gives to neighbor `j`. Row sums equal 1. If the influence graph is strongly connected and aperiodic, all opinions converge to consensus.

**When to use:** Baseline; testing pure network effects.

---

### Friedkin–Johnsen (Stubborn Agents)

```
x_i(t+1) = g_i × x_i(0)  +  (1 − g_i) × Σ_j  a_ij × x_j(t)
```

`g_i` is the self-weight (stubbornness). When `g_i = 0`, this reduces to DeGroot. When `g_i = 1`, this is a Zealot.

**When to use:** Most realistic for general users with prior beliefs. Default agent type for most runs.

---

### Hegselmann–Krause (Bounded-Confidence Agents)

```
N_i(t) = { j : |x_j(t) − x_i(t)| ≤ ε }   (set of "reachable" neighbors)

x_i(t+1) = (1 / |N_i(t)|) × Σ_{j ∈ N_i(t)}  x_j(t)
```

Agents only average neighbors within distance ε. Agents outside that window are completely ignored.

**When to use:** Simulating ideological filter bubbles driven by intolerance of difference.

---

### Voter Model (Binary Opinions)

At each tick, pick a random agent. That agent adopts the opinion of one randomly chosen neighbor. Opinions are binary (e.g., 0 or 1, or −1 and +1).

**When to use:** Simplified runs; studying absorption time to consensus; binary referendum scenarios.

---

### SIR Misinformation Spread

Agents exist in one of three states: **S**usceptible (haven't seen the claim), **I**nfected (believe/share it), **R**ecovered (saw it, rejected it or moved on).

```
S → I  with probability β  when a Susceptible agent sees an Infected neighbor's content
I → R  with probability γ  per tick (fact-checking rate / natural skepticism)
```

**Parameters:**
- `β` — transmission/infection rate per exposure (default: calibrated to empirical cascade data)
- `γ` — recovery/fact-check rate per tick (default: calibrated to empirical data)

**When to use:** Tracking spread of a specific false claim through the network.

## Part 6 — Emotion System

### Agent Arousal Update (per tick)

```
e_i(t+1) = λ × e_i(t)  +  (1 − λ) × v_c
```

- `e_i(t)` — current emotional arousal of agent i (range [0, 1])
- `λ` — emotional decay rate (default: **0.85**); how quickly arousal fades
- `v_c` — emotional valence of the content item consumed this tick

Misinformation content has higher average valence (sampled from Beta(3,1)). Neutral content has lower valence (Beta(1,3)).

### Arousal Effect on Sharing

```
P(share | c, i) = σ( base_share  +  w_e × e_i(t)  +  w_v × v_c )
```

- `σ` — sigmoid function (maps any value to [0,1] probability)
- `base_share` — baseline sharing rate (default: **−1.5** in log-odds, ~18% probability)
- `w_e` — weight of arousal (default: **0.3**)
- `w_v` — weight of content valence (default: **0.4**)

**Result:** High arousal + high valence content → significantly higher sharing probability.

### Arousal Effect on Opinion Openness

```
ε_eff(i, t) = ε × (1 − γ_e × e_i(t))
```

- `ε_eff` — the agent's effective confidence bound this tick (narrower when aroused)
- `γ_e` — emotion's grip on tolerance (default: **0.4**)

**Result:** A highly aroused agent becomes more closed-minded — their effective tolerance window shrinks, so they ignore more of their neighbors.

## Part 7 — Network Structure

### Topologies

| Topology | Model | Properties | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Small-world | Watts–Strogatz | High clustering, short path lengths; avg path ~5–10 | Realistic social networks |
| Scale-free | Barabási–Albert | Heavy-tailed degree distribution; a few hub nodes with huge reach | Modeling influencers and viral spread |
| Random | Erdős–Rényi | Uniform degree distribution; baseline/control | Control experiments only |
| Community | Stochastic Block Model | Pre-defined clusters with high within-group density | Pre-seeding group structure |

### Key Network Concepts

**Homophily:** The tendency of agents to connect with similar others. Controlled by `homophily_threshold` — the maximum opinion difference allowed when forming a new tie. Above 90% homophilous ties → strong echo chambers.

**Dynamic Rewiring:** Each tick, agents may unfollow disagreeing neighbors and/or follow more similar ones. This is driven by `rewire_probability` and `rewire_homophily_bias`.

**Assortativity:** A network-level measure of how much similar-opinion agents cluster together. Ranges from −1 (opposite opinions linked) to +1 (same opinions linked). Positive assortativity = echo chambers.

### Network Parameters

| Parameter | Description | Default |
|---|---|---|
| `N` | Number of agents | 1000 |
| `avg_degree` | Average number of connections per agent | 10–20 |
| `rewire_prob` | Watts–Strogatz rewiring probability | 0.1 |
| `homophily_threshold` | Max opinion gap for new tie formation | 0.2–0.4 |
| `rewire_probability` | Per-tick chance an agent rewires one edge | 0.01 |
| `multilayer` | Whether to use separate offline + online layers | False (single layer default) |

## Part 9 — Metrics

These are computed each tick (or at snapshot intervals). Use these to answer "how much of an echo chamber has formed?"

| Metric | Formula | What It Tells You |
|---|---|---|
| **Polarization Index** | Mean `|o_i − o_j|` across all edges | Average opinion gap between connected agents. Higher = more divided. |
| **Opinion Variance** | `(1/N) × Σ(o_i − ō)²` | Overall spread of opinions. 0 = consensus; high = fragmented. |
| **Opinion Entropy** | `−Σ p(o) × log p(o)` | Diversity of opinion distribution. Low = concentrated clusters. |
| **Assortativity (r)** | Pearson correlation of opinions across edges | +1 = only similar agents linked (echo chambers); −1 = cross-cutting. |
| **E-I Index** | `(external − internal) / (external + internal)` | −1 = all ties within group; +1 = all ties cross-group. |
| **Modularity (Q)** | Newman community score | 0.3–0.7 = strong community clusters (echo chambers). |
| **Misinformation Prevalence** | Fraction of agents in state I at tick t | 0–1; tracks spread of a specific false claim. |
| **Cascade Size** | Nodes exposed/sharing per episode | 0–100%; how far one piece of content travelled. |
| **Time to Convergence** | Ticks until opinion state stabilizes | Shorter = faster consensus or lock-in. |
| **Bot Prevalence** | Fraction of active agents flagged as bots | Tracks detection effectiveness over time. |
| **Exposure Disparity** | Δ misinformation exposure rate across ideological groups | Fairness metric; 0 = equal exposure across groups. |

### Intervention Effectiveness Score (IES)

When comparing a run with an intervention vs. a baseline control run:

```
ΔM = M_control(T) − M_intervention(T)
IES = ΔM / M_control(T)
```

An IES of 0.3 means the intervention reduced that metric by 30% relative to control.

## Part 10 — The Simulation Loop

Every tick, in order:

```
1. CONTENT GENERATION
   Each active agent may generate new content items
   (probability = activity_rate; content fields set at creation)

2. FEED GENERATION
   Recommender scores candidate content for each agent
   Top k_exp items selected based on similarity score + personalization α

3. CONTENT CONSUMPTION
   Each agent reads/processes items from their feed
   → emotional_arousal updated
   → pref_vector updated (exponential moving average)

4. SHARING DECISION
   For each consumed item: P(share) calculated using arousal formula
   Shared items enter neighbors' candidate pools next tick

5. OPINION UPDATE
   Each agent applies their update rule (DeGroot / FJ / HK / Voter)
   Using opinions of neighbors weighted by trust and recency
   SIR state transitions applied for misinformation tracking

6. NETWORK REWIRING (if enabled)
   Agents with dissatisfaction above threshold may unfollow + refollow

7. CHURN CHECK (if enabled)
   P(churn_i) calculated; churned agents set is_active = False
   Churned edges removed from graph

8. BOT DETECTION (every T_detect ticks)
   Suspicion scores recomputed; agents above s_thresh flagged

9. METRIC LOGGING
   All metrics computed and written to time-series log
```

## Part 11 — Behavior Quick-Reference for AI Assistants

Use this section to quickly answer common questions about agent behavior.

**"Will Agent X change their opinion if shown opposing content?"**
- Zealot: Never.
- Bot: Never.
- Stubborn (FJ): Partially — they drift toward neighbors but never fully abandon their initial belief.
- Flexible (DeGroot): Yes — fully averages toward neighbors, no memory.
- HK: Only if the opposing content is within ε of their current opinion.
- Contrarian: They'll move, but sometimes in the opposite direction of what you'd expect.

**"Why is misinformation spreading faster in this run?"**
Likely causes, in order of likelihood: (1) high `β` transmission rate, (2) Bot agents seeding it with high virality, (3) high `emotional_valence` in the content raising `emotional_arousal` and share probability, (4) high `α` personalization trapping users in ideologically aligned feeds, (5) low `media_literacy` across the population.

**"Why aren't echo chambers forming even with high homophily?"**
Check: (1) Is `ε` (confidence bound) too high? Agents may still listen to everyone. (2) Is `α` (personalization) near 0? The recommender may be injecting diversity. (3) Are there enough Flexible/DeGroot agents? They converge to consensus rather than clustering. (4) Is the network too sparse? Low degree = weak social influence per agent.

**"What's a realistic baseline run configuration?"**
N = 1000, d_avg = 16, Watts–Strogatz with p = 0.1, mixed agent population (60% Stubborn, 20% Flexible, 10% Passive, 5% Zealot, 5% Bot), ε = 0.3, α = 0.65, g_i ~ U(0.1, 0.3), T = 720 ticks, 10 Monte Carlo replicates.

**"What parameters have the biggest effect on polarization?"**
In roughly descending order of impact: `homophily_threshold`, `α` (personalization), `ε` (confidence bound), `stubbornness g_i`, fraction of Zealot/Bot agents, `β` (misinformation transmission rate).
